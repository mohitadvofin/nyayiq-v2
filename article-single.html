Section 74 CGST: When does non-payment become suppression?

18 APR 2026 BY ADV. MOHIT JAIN 8 MIN READ GST

The invocation of Section 74 of the CGST Act — which allows the department to recover tax "by reason of fraud, or wilful misstatement, or suppression of facts" — has been one of the most contested provisions in GST litigation. Recent Delhi High Court rulings have sharpened what constitutes "suppression" in this context.

The statutory framework

Section 74 contemplates extended period of five years from the relevant date, as against three years under Section 73. The precondition: tax must not have been paid or short-paid "by reason of fraud, wilful misstatement, or suppression of facts to evade tax."

The key phrase — "suppression of facts" — has been judicially interpreted to require a positive act of concealment, not mere omission.

Mere non-disclosure is not suppression. There must be an obligation to disclose, coupled with intent to evade.

The Delhi HC evolution

In Rajdhani Crafts Industries v. Commissioner CGST, the Delhi High Court held that where the assessee had duly filed GSTR-3B and GSTR-1 returns disclosing all outward supplies, the mere fact that self-assessment was later found incorrect does not constitute suppression.

This judgement builds on decades of pre-GST jurisprudence — Pushpam Pharmaceuticals, Continental Foundation — where the Supreme Court repeatedly held that "suppression" requires an element of positive concealment.

Practical implications

For practitioners, three takeaways emerge from this line of cases:

First, always examine whether the facts alleged to be "suppressed" were in fact disclosed in returns, e-way bills, or departmental audits. If so, the charge fails.

Second, extended period invocation requires a specific show-cause notice that particularizes the suppression. Generic allegations don't suffice.

Third, when challenging Section 74 demands in writ jurisdiction, frame the question as one of jurisdiction — not merely factual correctness.

The way forward

With the tax administration increasingly invoking Section 74 as the default provision for demand orders, practitioners must be prepared to challenge such invocations both at the adjudication stage and in appellate fora. The recent HC rulings provide powerful authority to limit the scope of "suppression" to cases of genuine concealment.

Related judgements cited

  • Rajdhani Crafts Industries v. Commissioner CGST (Delhi HC 2025)
  • Pushpam Pharmaceuticals v. CCE (SC 1995)
  • Continental Foundation v. CCE (SC 2007)
  • State of Gujarat v. Amba Industrial (SC 2024)
